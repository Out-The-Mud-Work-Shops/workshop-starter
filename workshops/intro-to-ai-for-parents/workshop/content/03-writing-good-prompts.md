The message you send an AI is called a **prompt**. The same question asked two
different ways can give you a so-so answer or a genuinely great one. Good news:
the "trick" is just being specific, the way you'd brief a helpful but
clueless-about-your-life assistant.

## The recipe: who, what, and how

A strong prompt usually says three things:

1. **Who it's for / who you are** — "I'm a parent of a shy 8-year-old…"
2. **What you want** — "…give me three ways to…"
3. **How you want it** — "…keep it short and use simple words."

## See the difference yourself

First, paste this vague version and read the reply:

```text
How do I talk to my kid about screen time?
```

Now paste this specific version and compare:

```text
I'm a parent of a 9-year-old who gets upset when screen time ends. Give me 3 short, calm phrases I can say in the moment, and 1 tip to set up before screen time even starts. Keep it practical.
```

The second answer is more useful because you gave it more to work with. **That's
the whole skill.**

## Your turn — a few to practice

Pick any of these, paste it in, then refine the reply by chatting back ("shorter",
"funnier", "for a teenager instead"):

```text
Plan a simple birthday party for a 6-year-old at home for under $100. List what to buy and a rough schedule.
```

```text
My child asked why the sky is blue. Explain it in a way a curious 7-year-old would enjoy, in about 4 sentences.
```

```text
Turn this to-do brain-dump into a tidy weekend plan: groceries, soccer practice 10am, fix the leaky tap, call mom, laundry, family movie night.
```

{{< note >}}
If an answer isn't quite right, you don't start over — you just **tell it what
to change**. Treating it like a conversation, not a search box, is the single
biggest upgrade to your results.
{{< /note >}}
